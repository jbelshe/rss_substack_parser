August 1st, 2022

I started writing this conclusion blog a little bit ago, on August 1st, 2025. Coincidentally, it was on August 1st, 2022, that the idea of this trip first materialized.

I can’t remember what compelled me to on August 1st, 2022, but on that date, I created a google doc file and titled it “The Great Escape.” While looking at google maps, I proceeded to create an itinerary for a tour of the world, spinning the map around on my browser and writing down into this doc every country that piqued my imagination.

Whether it was the post-grad existential dread, a covid-driven desire for revenge travel, or just a transient feeling of wanderlust, the idea of this trip entered this world on that day 3 years ago. And for these last 3 years, “The Great Escape” tab has been open on my computer in the background as a reminder of that dream.

While I didn’t make it through the entire initial list (which was initially an ambitious 5-continent and ~40-country itinerary), sitting here, writing this section in a cafe in Belgium with a flight home in a few days, I feel a sense of great pride, knowing that I actually did the damn thing.

I was scared and a little embarrassed about the idea of the trip at first. It wasn’t the traditional American path, and I didn’t know anyone who'd done the same before. It wasn’t until last year that I fully committed to making the dream trip become a reality, but in the 2.5 years of waiting in between, that tab on my computer has served as an aspiration and a bit of escapism from my daily life.

Well, after 2.5 years of glacial build-up, 6 months of backpacking have seemingly gone by in an instant. The dream turned into reality, and now that reality has come to a close. And now I’m left with the question: What comes next?

Back To Real-Life?

The trip began, and then it ended. Is it now time to just get back to normal life? Is this trip some sort of transient break from “real life” just to be resumed as if nothing happened?

When I was back home in April for halftime, I had a lot of people ask me if I thought I had changed at all. The answer I always gave was a pretty quick no. I still felt like the same person that I was before I started the trip. My morals hadn’t changed. I still had the same friends. Maybe I had some new interests and had learned quite a bit, but I still felt like who I was before I left. Looking back now, I don’t know if that’s the truth.

I remember a conversation I had in March with my travel friend/recurring blog character in the Asia adventures, Zach, while we were walking around a small lake in the Old Quarters of Hanoi. We were talking about travel and life. Many times throughout the last 7 months, I have found myself questioning what I’m doing in a place, or what’s the purpose of doing travel at all (yes, existentialism can hit on vacation too). Anyways, this was roughly what Zach and I were talking about, and I remember very distinctly Zach (an older and much more seasoned traveler than I) telling me:

“It’ll be years before you truly understand how travel has shaped your life.”

The idea here being that whether you realize it or not, an experience like this changes you, even if you don’t realize it at first or even maybe ever. It’ll show up in ways and at times that you don’t expect. Our lives are an accumulation of our experiences, and this trip has been a big chunk of mine.

I feel like I am the same person I was before the trip, but I don’t think that’s reality. If it was that would mean that coming home and resuming my life in the States wouldn’t be any different than how it was before, and I don’t want to let that happen.

A Moveable Feast

When I was in Paris, I read A Moveable Feast, Ernest Hemingway’s memoir about his time living in Paris in the 1920s. Hemingway didn’t finish it before his death, so the novel was initially untitled. His family chose the title “A Moveable Feast” for its posthumous publication, deriving the name from a remark his wife remembers Hemingway once making:

“If you are lucky enough to have lived in Paris as a young man, then wherever you go for the rest of your life, it stays with you, for Paris is a moveable feast.”

What does Hemingway mean here? A moveable feast, in the Church, typically refers to feast days that derive their calendar date based on when Easter is that year (the feast date moves each year). So, is Hemingway saying that Paris is a Christian holiday determined in reference to Easter? That doesn’t make a lot of sense.

Hemingway’s son writes an intro to the memoir, and speculates that Hemingway’s interpretation of “a moveable feast” was partially derived from a speech in Shakespeare's play Henry V , but that the phrase adopted a unique personal meaning for Hemingway. He notes that rather than a day/place of celebration, a moveable feast to Hemingway was,

“[A] memory or even a state of being that had become a part of you, a thing that you could have always with you, no matter where you went or how you lived forever after, that you could never lose. An experience first fixed in time and space or a condition like happiness or love could be afterward moved or carried with you wherever you went in space and time.”

Reading this helped me understand what comes next after this trip. I am still the same person I was before this trip, but I’m also different. You are your experiences, and while I’m going to resume life like I was living before I left on this trip, I will always have this trip with me. It’ll be the things I’ve seen, the people I’ve met, the history I’ve learned, and the memories (good and bad) I’ve made.

This adventure will forever be a moveable feast to me, sitting in the background of my mind, both subtly influencing me in ways I won’t always see and patiently waiting as a cherished memory to be tapped into on occasion. So, yes, life will go back to “normal” after this, but “normal” will never be the same.

Memories Fade, The Cloud Persists

And while some of these memories may eventually fade, thankfully, digital footprints are forever! This blog (while occasionally feeling like a chore to write) has been a very integral part of this trip, and I’m sure it is something I will be diving back into to reminisce over throughout my life. Thanks to everyone for falling along on this trip. Whoever you may be and whatever our relationship is (or even if we don’t know each other), I appreciate you coming along for the ride!

And that’s all he wrote,

Jack
